What is IH?

Idiopathic Hypersomnia (IH) is a neurological sleep disorder that can impact many aspects of a person’s life. This is a very rare disorder. IH often starts as a teenager or young adult and causes individuals to be excessively sleepy during the day even after a good or prolonged night’s sleep. Many have IH for many years without knowing it.

"Only 1 or 2 people for every 10,000 have IH."

Oftentimes individuals are misdiagnosed as having narcolepsy because IH and narcolepsy present in similar ways. The diagnosis of IH requires ruling out more common sleep disorders. If an underlying cause of hypersomnia cannot be found, a diagnosis of IH may be made based on an individual’s symptoms and the results of sleep tests.

Diagnosis.

Criteria for IH diagnosis.

  • Daily periods of excessive sleepiness for at least 3 months
  • No evidence of cataplexy or sudden muscle weakness
  • Results from a multiple sleep latency test (MSLT) show a sleep latency (time to fall asleep) of less than 8 minutes or a total sleep time of 11 or more hours.
  • Characteristic measurements of how long it takes to reach the REM sleep stage
  • Insufficient sleep syndrome is ruled out

The cause of IH is currently not known. It is hypothesized that it is related to chemical imbalances in the parts of the brain that regulate sleep and wakefulness.

Causes.

“Symptoms appear in a person’s teens or early twenties”

What IH looks like.

  • Feeling sleepy during the day and/or taking long naps
  • Sleeping for longer than usual at night (12-14 hours or longer)
  • Difficulty waking up
  • Problem making transition from being asleep to being awake (termed sleep inertia)
  • Sleep feels unrefreshing and non-restorative
  • Feelings of grogginess and disorientation upon waking up
  • Possible difficulty being able to work normally due to symptoms
  • The need for multiple alarm clocks and morning rituals to ensure individuals awake in time for life activities.

Since the cause of IH is unknown, the treatment is aimed at easing the symptoms that individuals have with this disorder. It is common practice to use medications that are known to be effective in people with narcolepsy and other neurologic/sleep disorders to treat the sleepiness associated with IH (this is called off-label use).

Treatment.

Treatment Options.

Medications may include stimulants such as: modafinil/amphetamine- like compounds, ADHD medications, antidepressants, and sedatives. The first U.S. FDA-approved treatment for IH, lower-sodium oxybate (or Xywav), was approved in August of 2021. Xywav is a medication taken at bedtime (and often with another dose later in the night) that promotes deep sleep and improved daytime sleepiness.

“A placebo-controlled study of more than 100 persons with IH showed that lower-sodium oxybate reduced sleepiness and other symptoms.”

Meet Cassie

How long have you known that you have IH?

"I was diagnosed when I was 21 years old in September of 2016."

What is it like living with IH? What are your day to day struggles?

"Before I was diagnosed, living with IH meant having a constant, overwhelming desire to be asleep. It didn’t matter where I was or what I was doing - part of me always wanted to be sleeping. I think the biggest struggle I had was waking up in the mornings. A symptom of IH is sleep inertia, or sleep drunkenness, which makes it extremely hard to wake up in the mornings or after a nap. In the past, I would have at least 10 alarms set each morning and sleep inertia would cause me to either sleep right through the alarms or wake up long enough to turn them off with no memory of doing so."

What was the diagnosis process like?

"I had a consultation appointment to talk to a doctor about the struggles I was having and my sleep habits. She then scheduled me for a sleep study a week later. I was hooked up to a bunch of wires on my face and chest and a tube up my nose and bedtime was 10pm. I was woken up at 7am and got the nose thing removed as well as some of the wires. The rest of the wires had to stay connected to me throughout the day for further testing. I got to sit in my room for about 2 hours before the nurse came back in and tucked me in for a 20 minute nap time. During this 20 minute period they monitored how quickly I fell asleep, how long I stayed asleep, and whether I entered the REM sleep cycle. This cycle was repeated 3 more times before I got to leave for the day."

"I went back to the office another week later to receive my test results. The doctor explained that the average person takes eight minutes or longer to fall asleep after they’ve laid down - anything less than eight minutes is considered abnormal. She then told me that during my 4 naps, I fell asleep within 2-5 minutes each time and stayed asleep until the nurse woke me up. Since I did not enter the REM sleep cycle at any point during my naps, narcolepsy was ruled out, but falling asleep as quickly and consistently as I did meant there was still an issue. My doctor then explained what IH is and that my sleepiness is still a medical issue but there’s no definite reason as to why I am so sleepy."

What do most not know about IH?

"That it exists."

What pushed you to receive medical help?

"I slept a lot throughout my childhood. Even as a baby, my mom told me she had to call the doctor to make sure it was normal that I was sleeping as much as I was. Falling asleep in class was a pretty normal thing for me throughout highschool and college. When I started working, I would set alarms and take 20-30 minute naps during my lunch break everyday. But I finally looked into sleep clinics the summer of 2016 because I noticed myself dozing off at red lights on my morning drive to work."
